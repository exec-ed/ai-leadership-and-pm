SCALE / PIVOT / KILL EXERCISE

Debrief Outline (3 minutes)

Purpose: Land the key learning without overthinking. Keep it tight.

DEBRIEF STRUCTURE

Opening (30 seconds)

“Notice what just happened: You analysed ambiguous data and made a business decision. That’s project management work. Perfect information doesn’t exist. You made the best call you could with what you had.”

Quick Poll (1 minute)

Ask the room: > “Hands up: How many groups chose SCALE?” > “How many chose PIVOT?” > “How many chose KILL?”

Note the distribution. (Most will choose Pivot—that’s the “right” answer with that data.)

One Follow-Up Question (1 minute)

If most chose PIVOT: > “Why pivot instead of scale? What data told you that?”

Let one group answer. (They’ll say: “We didn’t hit enough criteria” or “Gaps were solvable.”)

“Exactly. Notice: you let DATA, not hope, drive the decision.”

If groups split between Scale and Pivot: > “Some of you scaled, some pivoted. What’s the difference in how you read the data?”

Let them debate briefly. This reveals decision-making logic. Then:

“Both decisions can be defensible IF your reasoning is solid. Notice what matters: the quality of your thinking, not the choice itself.”

Landing the Learning (1 minute)

Pick ONE of these to emphasize:

Option A: Data vs. Gut Feel > “You had real numbers. You didn’t say ‘I think it’s ready.’ You said ‘Here’s what the data shows, so we’re [choosing].’ That’s how professionals decide.”

Option B: Solvable vs. Fundamental > “You distinguished between ‘this is broken’ and ‘this needs more time.’ That’s critical thinking. Not everything that’s not perfect is broken.”

Option C: Pivot is Normal > “Most of you chose Pivot—continue with improvements. That’s actually the most common decision in real projects. Scale and Kill are rarer. Pivot is how learning happens.”

Option D: Criteria Anchor > “Your original Go/No-Go criteria made this decision easier. You defined the bar upfront, then measured against it. That’s disciplined PM.”

Close (1 sentence)

“You just did what leaders do: made a hard call with imperfect information, defended it with data, and moved forward.”

TIMING NOTES

  • Don’t over-explain. This is a 3-minute debrief, not a 10-minute lecture.
  • Let groups speak. Ask questions, listen to their reasoning.
  • Notice patterns. What criteria mattered most? Where did they disagree?
  • Keep energy high. They just finished a lot of work—celebrate the thinking, not overthink the answers.

IF YOU HAVE TIME (5 minutes instead of 3)

Add this question:

“If you had to do this again tomorrow, what would you do differently?”

Let them reflect on: - What they’d gather more data on - How they’d involve stakeholders earlier - What criteria they’d change

This bridges to real-world application.
